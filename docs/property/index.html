Property & Land Records

Losing your papers is not losing your land.

Fire, flood, a family split, a house move — the papers go. People assume the property went with them. It didn't. The state keeps its own copy, permanently. Registration exists precisely so that a fire in your house cannot erase your title. Deeds from the 1920s are retrieved routinely.

Which record do I need? Find my state's portal
Start here if you've lost documents. Go to the sub-registrar's office where the deed was registered and ask for a certified copy. They hold a permanent record. A certified copy is legally usable in place of the original for sale, gift or a bank loan, provided you can account for the original's loss — usually with a police report. This one fact resolves most of the panic on this page.

Two systems. Most people only chase one.

This is the single most useful thing to understand about Indian land records, and almost nobody explains it. Two separate departments hold two different truths about your property, and you usually need both.

System 1 · Registration

The deed — who transferred it

Held by the sub-registrar / registration department. Records the transaction: sale, gift, partition, mortgage. This is where a certified copy of your deed and an Encumbrance Certificate come from.

Use it to ask: what deals have ever been registered on this property?

System 2 · Revenue

The record of rights — who holds it now

Held by the revenue department — tehsildar, patwari, anchal. Records who the state currently recognises as holder, and who pays the land revenue. This is the 7/12, RTC, patta, khatauni, khatiyan or jamabandi, depending on your state.

Use it to ask: whose name is on it today?

A registered deed does not update the revenue record automatically. That second step is mutation — and it is where inherited property goes wrong across generations. A family can hold a perfectly valid deed while the revenue record still names a man who died in 1954. Both records are "correct". They just disagree, and the disagreement is yours to fix.

The same document, twelve names

Land is a State subject, so each state named its records differently. Asking for the wrong word gets you a blank stare. Use your state's word.

Terminology varies within states too. If a term here doesn't match what your office uses, theirs is right — tell us and we'll fix it.

Start from what happened to you

Most common

"It's still in my grandfather's name"

You need the mutation done, and to prove the chain from the person named on the record down to you. In Bihar, a state-wide survey is running right now and there is a specific form for exactly this — with a deadline.

Bihar land survey guide →

Papers lost

"Everything burned / flooded"

File a police report, then apply to the sub-registrar for a certified copy. For deeds predating computerisation — roughly pre-1990, and certainly your grandfather's era — expect a physical search of the registry's ledgers, usually via a search advocate.

Before buying

"How do I know the seller owns it?"

Get an Encumbrance Certificate covering as many years back as they'll give, and check the revenue record separately. The two must agree. If they don't, walk away or get a lawyer — that gap is where fraud lives.

Stonewalled

"I applied and nothing happened"

An RTI asking who is holding your file, since when, and for the file notings tends to move things that polite follow-ups cannot. ₹10, 30-day statutory deadline.

RTI toolkit →

Occupied

"Someone else is on our land"

This is not a paperwork problem. Adverse possession and limitation periods may have run. Recovering the record and recovering the land are different fights, and the second has a clock. Talk to a lawyer before filing anything.

What we don't do, and why

We do not search land records for you, and we never will. We could build a national name-search. We decided not to. Today, finding someone's land holdings requires knowing their district, tehsil and village — that friction is doing protective work. A tool that collapses it helps an heir find their great-grandfather's khatiyan, and helps a predator find a widow's land just as efficiently. The obscurity is not an accident, and we won't be the ones to remove it.

We also don't mirror the portals. A copy of a live legal record goes stale, and someone would act on ours. Every link here goes to the state's own system, which is free, authoritative, and current.
